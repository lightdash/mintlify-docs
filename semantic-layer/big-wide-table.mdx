A semantic layer replaces the "throw everything into one table" approach with on-demand joins that fire only when needed.

<Info>**Status:** ✅ This is the whole point of the semantic layer.</Info>

## The question

> "I want analysts to break down any metric by any dimension — without a 50-column mega-table."

## Why it matters

Wide tables seem convenient until you maintain them. Every new column means reprocessing the entire table. Unrelated facts get cross-joined. Storage bloats. And when two fact tables meet in one row, metrics inflate (see [The Fan-Out Trap](/semantic-layer/fan-out-trap)). Lightdash's join system gives users the same "everything at your fingertips" experience, but only joins the tables each query actually touches.

## Example

An e-commerce company currently maintains this denormalized monster:

### The wide table (what you have today)

| order_id | order_date | order_total | customer_id | customer_name | plan_tier | signup_date | country | product_id | product_name | category | sku | supplier | ticket_id | ticket_date | priority |
|----------|------------|-------------|-------------|---------------|-----------|-------------|---------|------------|--------------|----------|-----|----------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| 501 | 2025-03-01 | 89.00 | 42 | Bolt Inc | Pro | 2024-06-15 | US | 301 | Widget A | Widgets | W-301 | Acme | 801 | 2025-03-02 | high |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

15+ columns. Every nightly rebuild takes 45 minutes and costs $12 in compute.

### The clean alternative: 3 small tables + joins

**orders** (4 columns)

| order_id | customer_id | product_id | order_total |
|----------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| 501 | 42 | 301 | 89.00 |
| 502 | 42 | 302 | 134.50 |
| 503 | 17 | 301 | 67.00 |

**customers** (5 columns)

| customer_id | name | plan_tier | signup_date | country |
|-------------|----------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| 17 | Acme Corp | Free | 2024-01-10 | UK |
| 42 | Bolt Inc | Pro | 2024-06-15 | US |

**products** (4 columns)

| product_id | product_name | category | sku |
|------------|--------------|----------|-------|
| 301 | Widget A | Widgets | W-301 |
| 302 | Gadget B | Gadgets | G-302 |

### YAML

```yaml
version: 2
models:
  - name: orders
    meta:
      primary_key: order_id
      joins:
        - join: customers
          sql_on: ${orders.customer_id} = ${customers.customer_id}
          relationship: many-to-one
        - join: products
          sql_on: ${orders.product_id} = ${products.product_id}
          relationship: many-to-one
    columns:
      - name: order_total
        meta:
          metrics:
            total_revenue:
              type: sum
              format: "usd"
            order_count:
              type: count

  - name: customers
    meta:
      primary_key: customer_id
    columns:
      - name: plan_tier
        meta:
          dimension:
            type: string
      - name: country
        meta:
          dimension:
            type: string

  - name: products
    meta:
      primary_key: product_id
    columns:
      - name: category
        meta:
          dimension:
            type: string
```

### Result

An analyst selects `country` and `total_revenue`. Lightdash joins only `orders → customers` — the `products` table is never touched:

| country | total_revenue |
|---------|---------------|
| US | $142,380 |
| UK | $89,210 |

A different analyst selects `category` and `order_count`. Now Lightdash joins only `orders → products`:

| category | order_count |
|----------|-------------|
| Widgets | 1,204 |
| Gadgets | 876 |

Same Explore, different queries, different joins. No 15-column rebuild required.

## How to set it up

1. Break your wide table into normalized models in dbt — one per entity (orders, customers, products).
2. Declare `primary_key` on each model.
3. Define joins with the correct `relationship` (`many-to-one` for lookups, `one-to-many` for fact tables).
4. Users open one Explore and see dimensions from all joined tables — Lightdash only fires the joins their query needs.
5. For fact-to-fact joins (e.g. orders + tickets through customers), use `one-to-many` on both sides so symmetric aggregates kick in.
